  Reviewer Role: Editor-in-Chief

  ---
  Overall Assessment

  Recommendation: ☑ Major Revision
  Confidence Score: 4

  Summary: The paper proposes Q-FENG, a neurosymbolic governance monitoring
  architecture operationalized through a 5-stage pipeline (E0–E4), and validates it
  across 7 formal scenarios spanning three normative regimes (Brazilian healthcare,
  US/EU healthcare, and Brazilian labour law). The core technical contribution —
  modelling governance alignment as a quantum interference angle θ between predictor
  preference vectors and normative state vectors — is original and formally grounded.
  The Markovian theta-efetivo extension and the Born-rule quantum vs. classical
  comparison add formal depth. The Manaus COVID-19 case study, using real SIH/DATASUS
  microdata, provides a compelling empirical anchor.

  However, the paper's contribution claims exceed what a 7-scenario PoC can support.
  The absence of comparison with existing governance monitoring tools (even simple
  baselines such as rule-based checkers or thresholded ML classifiers) makes it
  impossible to assess the practical advantage of the quantum interference formalism
  over simpler alternatives. The single-annotator HITL stage (§7.4) is a significant
  validity concern that the paper acknowledges but does not adequately mitigate. The
  "quantum" terminology will attract scrutiny in an AI engineering venue; §7.1 is
  proactive but may not satisfy specialist reviewers. Journal fit for Applied
  Intelligence is good, but the paper needs a clearer statement of its target
  readership contribution.

  Recommendation rationale: Major Revision — the technical foundations are sound, the
  empirical case study is genuine, but the paper needs (1) a baseline comparison, (2)
  improved HITL validity discussion, and (3) tightened contribution claims calibrated
  to PoC scope.

  ---
  Strengths

  S1: Original Quantum Interference Formalism
  The interference angle θ = arccos(⟨ψ_N|ψ_S⟩) is a genuinely original scalar measure
  for governance alignment. The formal connection to quantum cognition (Busemeyer and
  Bruza 2012; Pothos and Busemeyer 2013) is appropriately cited, and the mathematical
  derivation is self-contained. The bimodal empirical distribution — CB scenarios in
  [127.8°, 134.7°], STAC in [5.6°, 7.1°] — is a compelling validation of the threshold
  partition.

  S2: Real Administrative Microdata for Manaus
  The use of SIH/DATASUS microdata (1,526 COVID-19 admissions, Oct/2020–Mar/2021) is a
  substantive empirical contribution that distinguishes this paper from purely
  theoretical governance frameworks. The early CB activation in October 2020 (θ_eff =
  125.34°), three months before the Portaria 69/2021 emergency declaration, provides a
  concrete and independently verifiable benchmark.

  S3: Failure Typology with Legal Grounding
  The three-category failure taxonomy (constitutional / execution-absent-channel /
  execution-inertia) derived from sovereign predicate analysis is a practical
  contribution. Each failure type maps to a distinct intervention class (legislative /
  operational / citation-grounding), which is operationally useful beyond the academic
  context.

  S4: Statistical Robustness Battery
  The three complementary analyses — threshold robustness (245 parameter combinations,
  98.4% correct), ψ-sensitivity (n=500 per scenario, ±20% perturbation), and bootstrap
  CIs — constitute a credible robustness validation for a PoC. Few AI governance papers
   include this level of statistical diligence.

  S5: Book Integration and Theoretical Grounding
  §2.0 and §7.5 successfully position the paper within a three-document research
  sequence (Kaminski 2026a → this paper → Paper 2). The tripartite governance taxonomy
  and the Fricção Ontológica concept provide a theoretical foundation that elevates the
   paper above a pure engineering report.

  ---
  Weaknesses

  W1: No External Baseline Comparison
  Problem: §7.1 (p. [Discussion]) acknowledges that "a broader comparison against
  external governance monitoring tools (e.g., Fairlearn, IBM AI Fairness 360) is
  planned for the second paper." However, the absence of any external baseline — even a
   simple rule-based checker that fires when any sovereign predicate is violated —
  makes it impossible to assess whether the quantum interference formalism provides
  advantages over simpler approaches. The GSP (9.4–25.2%) measures difference from an
  internal classical Bayesian model, not from real-world alternatives.
  Why it matters: Without a baseline, the "quantum advantage" is a mathematical
  property of the model, not an empirically demonstrated improvement over alternatives.
   A referee will ask: "Could a threshold-based system using the same Clingo
  predicates, without any quantum formalism, achieve the same governance
  classification?"
  Suggestion: Add at minimum one ablation: a rule-based baseline that classifies
  governance failure solely by sovereign predicate count (CB if N_sovereign_active >
  threshold, STAC otherwise), and compare its regime classification against Q-FENG.
  This would demonstrate that the interference geometry adds information beyond
  predicate counting.
  Severity: Major

  W2: Single-Annotator HITL — Validity Concern
  Problem: §7.4 acknowledges that "the sovereignty classifications in the current PoC
  were reviewed by a single annotator (the author) with legal training." Given that θ
  is a direct function of ψ_S, which is determined by sovereignty classifications, and
  ψ_S is determined by HITL judgments, the validation loop is: author annotates → θ is
  computed → author validates paper → paper cites θ. The SOVEREIGN/ELASTIC decisions
  made by the author are unverified by independent legal expertise.
  Why it matters: If the HITL classifications are systematically biased toward
  producing CB outcomes in the "expected" scenarios, the reported θ values are not
  evidence of governance failure detection but of annotation preferences. The bootstrap
   CIs address statistical noise but not annotation bias.
  Suggestion: Report inter-annotator reliability for at least a subset of predicates
  (Cohen's κ with a second legal expert), or clearly limit the paper's claims to
  "design validation" (showing the system can classify) rather than "empirical
  validation" (showing the system correctly classifies).
  Severity: Major

  W3: PoC Scope vs. Contribution Claims
  Problem: The abstract and §8 use language like "empirical validation... across three
  normative regimes" and "continuous prospective governance." The actual scope is 7
  designed scenarios, 4 of which are in a single legal domain (labour law) with a
  single precedent case (Mata v. Avianca). The three "regimes" share an author and
  design team. This does not constitute validation in the hypothesis-testing sense.
  Why it matters: Applied Intelligence readers expect either (a) a sufficiently large
  and diverse validation set that supports generalization claims, or (b) language
  explicitly limiting claims to "proof-of-concept demonstration." The current framing
  sits between these two, creating a credibility risk.
  Suggestion: Consistently frame the paper as "PoC demonstration" in all contribution
  claims. Replace "empirical validation" with "proof-of-concept empirical
  demonstration" in abstract and conclusions. This is not a limitation — it is honest
  scope management that strengthens rather than weakens the paper.
  Severity: Major

  ---
  Detailed Comments

  Title & Abstract: Title is clear and accurate. "Three normative regimes" is slightly
  misleading — the labour law scenarios (T-CLT-01 to 04) test the same legal instrument
   (CLT/TST) in variations, not a distinct normative regime from constitutional health
  law. Consider: "two normative domains and three institutional contexts." Abstract:
  the 300-word abstract is strong; add one sentence on the external baseline limitation

  Introduction: The §1.7 "fulfillment" framing (this paper delivers what Kaminski 2026a
   promised) is intellectually honest but may read as self-congratulatory without
  Kaminski 2026a being peer-reviewed. Suggest a lighter touch: "the present paper
  provides the formal computational demonstration of the theoretical framework
  developed in [author's prior work, 2026a]."

  §5 Validation Results: Strong. The Manaus narrative (§5.3) is the most compelling
  part of the paper. Consider making it the opening case study rather than the third
  one, as it provides the most concrete and independently grounded evidence.

  §7 Discussion: §7.1 is proactive and well-argued. The disclaimer about "quantum
  mathematics as modelling language" is necessary and appropriate. Consider also
  addressing: if the quantum mathematics can be approximated by cosine similarity
  (which it can, since θ is derived from the inner product), what does the additional
  formalism add beyond a cosine-similarity classifier?

  References: The new references (Bromley & Powell, DiMaggio & Powell, Power, Meyer &
  Rowan) are appropriate but the paper needs to also cite Callon more explicitly in the
   main text where market-device performativity is relevant.

  📋 Peer Review Report — Peer Reviewer 1 (Methodology)

  Reviewer Role: Peer Reviewer 1 — Methodology
  Reviewer Identity: Associate Professor, Logic Programming and Formal Verification; PI
   on ERC ASP compliance project
  Review Focus: Mathematical soundness of quantum formalism; ASP/Clingo methodology;
  statistical validity
  Review Date: 2026-04-20 | Round: 1

  ---
  Overall Assessment

  Recommendation: ☑ Major Revision
  Confidence Score: 5

  Summary: The paper introduces a formally coherent framework for modelling governance
  alignment using quantum interference geometry. I reviewed the mathematical
  foundations (§3), the Clingo/ASP methodology (§4), and the statistical analyses (§6)
  with full domain competence. The interference angle θ formulation is mathematically
  sound, the Markovian recurrence is well-constructed, and the Born-rule comparison
  against a classical mixture model is correctly derived. The Clingo
  constraint-stripping approach for UNSAT programs is an elegant solution to the
  semantic extraction problem.

  However, three methodological concerns require Major Revision. First, the Born-rule
  comparison baseline is internally defined rather than externally benchmarked — the
  classical model is constructed to be contrasted with the quantum model, but this
  comparison does not demonstrate advantage over real-world alternatives. Second, the
  single-annotator HITL creates an unquantified source of systematic variance in all
  reported θ values. Third, the anticipatory form (Eq. 5, γ > 0) is formally proved but
   never implemented — its inclusion in the formalism without empirical testing is
  misleading. The statistical robustness analyses are appropriate, but the sensitivity
  analysis lacks a proper uncertainty propagation path from annotation variance to θ
  variance.

  ---
  Strengths

  S1: Mathematical Consistency of Quantum Formalism
  The formalism from Equation 1 (θ = arccos(⟨ψ_N|ψ_S⟩)) through Equation 11 (cybernetic
   loss) is internally consistent. The normalisation guarantee (both vectors
  unit-length) is correctly maintained throughout. The Born-rule derivation (Eqs. 6–10)
   correctly follows Busemeyer and Bruza (2012), and the interference cross-term Z = 1
  + 2αβcos(θ) is correctly identified as the structural difference from a classical
  Bayesian mixture.

  S2: Clingo Two-Pass Strategy for UNSAT Programs
  The constraint-stripping approach — running a second Clingo pass with integrity
  constraints removed to extract active atoms from semantically unsatisfiable programs
  — is methodologically sound and not trivially obvious. Most ASP compliance-checking
  papers avoid UNSAT programs entirely; this paper correctly handles them. The failure
  typology (constitutional / execution-absent-channel / execution-inertia) maps cleanly
   onto the ASP semantics.

  S3: Bootstrap CI with Propagation to Source Uncertainty
  The bootstrap CI analysis (§6.3) appropriately distinguishes CI widths between
  SIH-derived months (±1–2°) and literature-estimated months (±3–4°), correctly
  reflecting the higher uncertainty of imputed data. The 10,000 bootstrap replications
  are adequate for the sample sizes involved.

  S4: Threshold Robustness Design
  Testing across 245 parameter combinations (7 values of θ_block × 5 values of θ_stac ×
   7 scenarios) is thorough. The 98.4% success rate with failures concentrated at
  θ_block = 130° for T-CLT-02 (θ = 127.81°, the nearest CB case to the threshold) is
  precisely the expected failure pattern, confirming the analysis was implemented
  correctly.

  ---
  Weaknesses

  W1: Born-Rule Classical Baseline Is Structurally Constructed, Not Empirically
  Grounded
  Problem: The classical Bayesian model used for GSP computation (P_cl = α²ψ_N[0]² +
  β²ψ_S[0]²) is defined as a mixture model without cross-terms — it is the quantum
  model minus the interference term. This is mathematically valid as a contrast, but it
   is not a baseline model that anyone would actually deploy for governance monitoring.
   The GSP of 9.4–25.2% measures the difference between two abstract models, not
  between Q-FENG and a real comparator.
  Why it matters: The paper cannot claim "the quantum interference model outperforms
  classical Bayesian approaches" without testing against an actual classical Bayesian
  implementation (e.g., a Naïve Bayes classifier trained on predicate activation
  patterns, or a logistic regression model). The internal contrast is technically
  correct but practically uninformative as a benchmark.
  Suggestion: Either (a) test against a real external classifier on the same 7
  scenarios, or (b) reframe GSP as a "structural sensitivity measure" — the degree to
  which the quantum formalism generates lower violation probabilities for a given θ —
  without claiming this as an "advantage" over classical methods.
  Severity: Major

  W2: Anticipatory Form (Eq. 5) Is Not Implemented — Should Be Removed or Tested
  Problem: Equation 5 (θ_eff with γ > 0 anticipatory term) and Appendix B.3 (formal
  proof of early activation property) describe a capability that is explicitly stated
  as unimplemented: "The PoC implementation uses γ = 0." Including an unimplemented
  equation as a formal contribution creates an incomplete paper structure.
  Why it matters: Readers and reviewers cannot evaluate whether the anticipatory form
  provides empirical benefits if it has never been tested. Appendix B.3's proof is
  valid, but a proof without empirical instantiation is not a validated contribution.
  Suggestion: Either (a) implement γ > 0 for the Manaus series (this is computationally
   straightforward given the existing pipeline) and report results; or (b) move Eq. 5
  and Appendix B.3 to a "Future Directions" section explicitly labeled as unvalidated
  extensions.
  Severity: Major

  W3: Annotation Variance Not Propagated to Reported θ
  Problem: The ψ-sensitivity analysis (§6.2) perturbs ψ-weights by ±20% and reports
  stability. However, the ψ_S vector is constructed from Clingo active atoms, which
  depend on SOVEREIGN/ELASTIC classifications from the HITL stage. A SOVEREIGN→ELASTIC
  misclassification would reduce the weight of that predicate in ψ_S, changing θ. The
  paper does not quantify how much annotation error translates to θ variance.
  Why it matters: If a single SOVEREIGN→ELASTIC misclassification changes θ from 132°
  (CB) to 118° (HITL), the governance regime classification changes — but this is not
  captured by the ψ perturbation analysis, which perturbs existing weights rather than
  removing predicates.
  Suggestion: Add a "leave-one-predicate-out" sensitivity analysis: for each scenario,
  remove one SOVEREIGN predicate at a time and report the resulting θ. If scenarios are
   robust to single-predicate removal, this strengthens confidence in the HITL
  classifications; if not, it quantifies the annotation risk.
  Severity: Major

  ---
  Detailed Comments

  §3.1: The regime table (STAC/HITL/CB) is well-specified. The claim that the 30°/120°
  thresholds "were determined by regime-semantics calibration" is vague — on what data?
   The 7 scenarios themselves? If so, this is circular (thresholds set to match
  observed data). Clarify the threshold-setting procedure.

  §3.3: The Born-rule derivation is correct. However, Equation 7 defines the
  superposition state as |Ψ_governance⟩ = α|ψ_N⟩ + β|ψ_S⟩, but ψ_N and ψ_S are real
  vectors, not quantum states. The "ket" notation is suggestive of quantum mechanics
  but technically incorrect for real Euclidean vectors. This is not a mathematical
  error but a notational choice that will attract criticism from physicists and quantum
   computing specialists. Consider using explicit vector notation throughout.

  §6.1: The threshold robustness analysis says "4 failures only at θ_block = 130° for
  T-CLT-02." This is because T-CLT-02 has θ = 127.81° — literally below the boundary.
  This is not a robustness failure; it is the expected behavior of a hard threshold.
  The 98.4% statistic is therefore not evidence of robustness so much as evidence that
  most scenarios are far from the boundary. Report distance-to-threshold as a separate
  metric.

  Appendix B: B.1 proof is correct. B.2 monotonicity proof has the caveat that the
  C2/C7 ordering in Table 4 violates monotonicity — this is a partial invalidation of
  the claim. Flag this explicitly in the body of the paper, not just the appendix.

  The paper's central empirical claim is that Q-FENG detects governance failures

  author who designed the CB scenarios. The bimodal distribution of θ (CB cluster vs.

  discriminative validity.

  what it was designed to classify, and detects failures it was trained to detect. This

  demonstrate the system; it is wrong to call the demonstration "validation."

  Ignored Alternative Explanations/Paths

  1. Cosine similarity classifier: The interference angle θ = arccos(⟨ψ_N|ψ_S⟩) is
  mathematically identical to the cosine distance between ψ_N and ψ_S. A governance
  monitoring system based on cosine similarity between a "preferred action vector" and
  a "normative action vector" would classify regimes identically to Q-FENG without any
  quantum formalism. The paper should demonstrate why the quantum Born-rule computation
   adds governance value beyond what simple cosine-similarity thresholding provides.
  2. Predicate violation counting: The simplest governance monitoring approach would
  be: count the number of active sovereign predicates whose preferred action is blocked
   by the predictor; if count > threshold, trigger circuit breaker. This rule-based
  approach requires no quantum formalism and might achieve the same classification on
  the 7 scenarios. The paper provides no evidence that it wouldn't.
  3. Standard ASP compliance checking: The existing literature on ASP-based compliance
  checking (Governatori, Palmirani) already provides formal methods for detecting
  normative violations. Q-FENG's contribution over these approaches — specifically the
  quantum interference layer — is not demonstrated through comparison.

  ---
  Unexamined Premise

  The paper assumes that detecting governance failures through θ > 120° is equivalent
  to preventing them. This assumption underlies the entire "compliance-by-construction"
   framing. But a governance monitoring system that fires a "Circuit Breaker" alert is
  only valuable if (a) someone acts on the alert in time, (b) that someone has the
  authority to halt the flagged process, and (c) the alert is trustworthy enough to
  warrant intervention costs. The paper does not examine any of these conditions. The
  unstated premise is: "governance detection → governance improvement," but this causal
   chain requires a theory of institutional action that the paper does not provide.

  - The Appendix B mathematical proofs, while their scope is limited, add genuine
  formal value that most AI governance papers lack.
  - The Markovian theta_efetivo formulation is the most original mathematical
  contribution — its adaptive memory design is elegant and genuinely applicable beyond
  the governance domain.

  Decision Rationale

  The panel unanimously recognizes Q-FENG as a technically ambitious and
  interdisciplinary contribution at the intersection of neurosymbolic AI, quantum
  decision theory, formal legal reasoning, and AI governance. The Markovian
  theta_efetivo formalism is the paper's most original mathematical contribution. The
  Manaus case study is the paper's most compelling empirical anchor. The tripartite
  governance taxonomy and the Fricção Ontológica concept (from the book integration)
  provide rare sociological depth in an AI engineering paper.

  However, two issues prevent publication in current form. First, the validation
  circularity identified by the DA (DA-C1, DA-C2) is a genuine concern: the author
  designed, annotated, and validated the 7 scenarios. This is not misconduct — it is
  standard PoC methodology — but it requires the paper to consistently use PoC framing
  rather than "empirical validation" language throughout. Second, the absence of any
  external baseline (DA, EIC, R1) means the quantum interference formalism cannot be
  demonstrated as superior to simpler alternatives. Even a single ablation comparison
  (rule-based predicate counter, or cosine-similarity classifier) would address this.

  The DA's two CRITICAL findings (DA-C1: bimodal distribution by design; DA-C2:
  circular validation loop) are real but addressable through honest framing — they do
  not indicate that the paper's contributions are false, only that the current language
   overstates their independent evidentiary status. This is a framing revision, not a
  methodology overhaul.

  Total estimated revision effort: 3–4 weeks for a disciplined author.
