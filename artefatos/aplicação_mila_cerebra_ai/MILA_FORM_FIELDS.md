Democratic Accountability for Algorithmic Governance: A Neurosymbolic Framework for Diagnosing AI Policy Failures

---

## 2. Project Summary (max 100 words) — 91 words

Current AI governance frameworks operate through post-hoc verification—audits, checklists, impact assessments—without mechanisms to verify whether their normative objectives are computationally achievable. A comparative analysis of 34 governance documents across 10 jurisdictions reveals that zero frameworks embed normative constraints architecturally. This project develops a policy brief introducing a formal typology of AI governance failures—execution versus constitutional—grounded in empirical evidence from public health (Brazil SUS, U.S. Medicaid algorithms) and comparative policy analysis (fsQCA). Drawing on the Q-FENG neurosymbolic framework, the brief demonstrates how democratic accountability over algorithmic systems can be made computationally verifiable.

## 3. Project Rationale and Objectives (max 350 words) — 302 words

AI governance frameworks are performative devices: they constitute the space of algorithmic behaviors rather than merely regulating it. When a framework defines risk categories or mandates transparency, it shapes what counts as compliant and whose interests become institutionally visible. Governance failures are therefore democratic deficits—breakdowns in institutional capacity to retain normative authority over algorithmic tools.

A comparative study I conducted—34 AI governance documents across 10 jurisdictions, 238 structured extractions across 7 analytical dimensions using fuzzy-set QCA—confirms this deficit is systemic. Frameworks converge on procedural requirements but lack mechanisms to verify whether normative objectives are computationally achievable. The analysis produced a tripartite taxonomy: Type I (principled-declaratory, e.g. OECD Principles), Type II (procedural-organizational, e.g. EU AI Act, NIST AI RMF), and a structurally absent Type III (architectural-computational governance). Zero documents achieved Type III. The governance layer dimension emerged as a necessary condition (inclusion = 1.000), yet the parsimonious solution (dim1 + normative formalization + performativity recognition) reveals that governance intent is necessary but insufficient without architectural embedding capacity.
Two empirical cases illustrate concrete consequences. In Brazil's 2021 Manaus oxygen crisis, the health data infrastructure contained sufficient signals to anticipate the catastrophe, yet no mechanism translated convergent data into emergency escalation—an execution failure. In a structurally distinct case, Obermeyer et al. (2019) showed a U.S. Medicaid algorithm systematically underestimated Black patients' needs by optimizing expenditure rather than clinical severity—a constitutional failure where equitable outcomes were architecturally impossible. These are health cases, but the governance gap applies across domains: social protection, agriculture, food security, and education all deploy algorithms within normative frameworks lacking formal alignment verification.

Objectives: (a) introduce a formal typology of AI governance failures grounded in comparative evidence; (b) demonstrate how neurosymbolic audit architecture makes democratic accountability computationally verifiable; (c) provide actionable recommendations for regulators closing the gap between governance intent and governance capacity.

## 4. Methodology and Approach (max 250 words) — 233 words

The policy brief integrates three evidentiary streams. First, the comparative fsQCA analysis (34 documents, 10 jurisdictions, 7 dimensions) provides the empirical governance gap diagnosis, establishing the tripartite taxonomy and identifying structural absence of Type III governance. Second, contrafactual Q-FENG validation using publicly available data—DATASUS (SINAN, SIH, CNES) for the Manaus case and the Obermeyer et al. synthetic dataset for the Medicaid case—demonstrates how execution and constitutional failures manifest concretely. Third, the EU AI Act's regulatory architecture is analyzed as a normative digestion pipeline to identify operationalization gaps between legislative intent and computational enforcement.

The Q-FENG framework formalizes the tension between AI inference and institutional norms as Ontological Friction, measured by a continuous angular metric (θ). This enables diagnostic precision (distinguishing failure types), normative explainability (identifying which specific norm was violated), and ontological sovereignty (computationally guaranteeing that democratic norms cannot be optimized away). The brief translates these formal properties into policy-accessible language with concrete institutional design recommendations.

The policy roundtable will convene stakeholders from the OECD AI and Health Expert Group (AIHEG)—of which I am a member—and from the BRICS+ health digital governance initiative, where I serve as technical focal point for Brazil's Ministry of Health. While empirical anchors come from health, the roundtable addresses democratic governance of algorithmic systems across policy domains. The dissemination strategy targets the OECD working group, the Brazilian federal policy community, and the AI governance research community via SSRN.
